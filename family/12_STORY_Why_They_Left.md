## The Answer: They Had No Choice

You're an American. But every one of your 72,182 documented ancestors was European — English, Scottish, Welsh, Swedish, French, Irish, Dutch, German, Italian, Spanish. For over a thousand years, they lived, worked, married, and died within a few miles of where they were born.

Then, in the span of three generations, they left. All of them. And they never went back.

This is the story of why.

## Chapter 1: The Cotton Trap (Lancashire, 1760-1860)

Your Parkinson line lived in the heart of the [Industrial Revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Industrial_Revolution) — not as its beneficiaries, but as its raw material.

**[Barrowford](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barrowford)**, Lancashire. A village in the shadow of [Pendle Hill](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pendle_Hill). By the time your ancestor **Henry Webster Parkinson** (b. 1768) was growing up, the [Lancashire cotton industry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lancashire_cotton_industry) was transforming the countryside into the world's first industrial landscape:

- By 1860, there were **2,650 cotton mills** in Lancashire employing **440,000 people**
- Lancashire produced **half the world's cotton**
- Workers — including children as young as 5 — labored **12-16 hour days**
- [Child labor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Child_labour#Industrial_Revolution) was standard. Children crawled beneath running machines to tie broken threads.
- [Cholera](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cholera) swept through mill towns repeatedly (1831, 1849, 1854)
- Average life expectancy in industrial Lancashire: **17 years** for laborers

Your ancestor **William Parkinson** (b. 1800, [Briercliffe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Briercliffe)) died at just **31 years old** in 1831 — the same year his son Samuel was born. Whether it was disease, accident, or exhaustion, the cotton mills killed young men.

Then came the [Lancashire Cotton Famine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lancashire_Cotton_Famine) (1861-1865). When the American Civil War cut off raw cotton imports, Lancashire's mills went silent. **400,000 workers were unemployed.** Families starved. The British government organized relief, but the damage was done — people started leaving.

**Samuel Rose Parkinson** (b. 1831, Barrowford) made the decision his father couldn't. He left Lancashire for America. He was part of a wave — between 1850 and 1900, over **4 million English people emigrated**, most to America, Canada, and Australia.

**He traded Pendle Hill for the Snake River Valley. Cotton mills for open sky. A 17-year life expectancy for 88 years of living.** He died in 1919 in Preston, Idaho — an old man in a new world his father never imagined.

## Chapter 2: The Cornish Exodus (Cornwall, 1830-1900)

Your grandmother **Bessie Ann Doney** (b. 1875, Franklin, Idaho) carried a Cornish name. The Doneys came from [Cornwall](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cornwall) — the far southwestern tip of England, a peninsula of granite, moors, and mine shafts.

Cornwall's story is tin. For **4,000 years**, Cornish miners dug tin and copper from the earth. The Romans came for it. The Phoenicians may have traded for it. By the 1800s, Cornwall was the world's largest producer of copper and tin.

Then it collapsed.

- **1830s-1870s:** Cheaper copper discovered in Chile, Australia, and Michigan undercut Cornish mines
- **Tin prices crashed** as Southeast Asian deposits flooded the market
- **250,000 Cornish people emigrated** between 1815 and 1920 — from a county of only ~350,000
- They were called **"[Cousin Jacks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cornish_diaspora)"** — showing up wherever there were mines: California, Montana, Wisconsin, Michigan, Australia, South Africa

The [Cornish diaspora](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cornish_diaspora) was one of the great scatterings of the 19th century. A mining people without mines. They took their skills — hard rock drilling, tunnel engineering, ore processing — and went wherever the earth needed opening.

**Sarah Mutton Doney** (b. 1835, [St. Stephens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/St_Stephen-in-Brannel), Cornwall) was part of this exodus. She died in Holyoke, Massachusetts — 3,000 miles from the granite coast where her family had lived for generations.

Her descendants married into the Parkinsons in Franklin, Idaho. Two families pushed out of England by different industrial forces — cotton and tin — converging in the American West.

## Chapter 3: The Island They Couldn't Feed (Gotland, 1850-1910)

While the Parkinsons fled cotton mills and the Doneys fled collapsed mines, your maternal line faced a different crisis on a [Baltic Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baltic_Sea) island 1,000 miles to the northeast.

[Gotland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gotland), Sweden. A limestone island, flat and fertile, but finite. By the mid-1800s, Gotland was running out of room:

- **Population grew faster than the land could support**
- Farms were subdivided into smaller and smaller plots with each generation
- [Crop failures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swedish_emigration_to_the_United_States) in 1867-1869 pushed families to the brink
- The [Swedish class system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_classes_in_Sweden) was rigid — if you were born a farmer's child, you died a farmer's child
- The [Lutheran State Church](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Church_of_Sweden) controlled religious life. Dissenters had no options.

Between 1850 and 1910, **1.3 million Swedes emigrated** — roughly **20% of the entire population.** From Gotland specifically, thousands left for the American Midwest and Mountain West. Many were recruited by [LDS missionaries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Church_of_Jesus_Christ_of_Latter-day_Saints_in_Sweden) who traveled to Scandinavia in the 1870s-1890s.

**Jacob Ludwig Ostberg** (b. 1846, [Vamlingbo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vamlingbo), Gotland) made the decision. He brought his family — including his daughter **Anna Maria Carolina Katrina Ostberg** (b. 1872, Hamra) — from the southern parishes of Gotland to the mountain valleys of Idaho and Utah.

Anna Maria married Alexander Smith Coleman in Plano, Idaho. She lived to be 82 — dying in the same community she'd helped build, 5,000 miles from the Baltic island where eight generations of her maternal ancestors had lived.

## Chapter 4: The Puritan Gamble (England → Massachusetts, 1620-1640)

Long before the Industrial Revolution pushed your ancestors out, **religion pulled them across.**

Between 1620 and 1640, over **20,000 English Puritans** sailed for [Massachusetts Bay Colony](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Massachusetts_Bay_Colony) in what historians call the [Great Migration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Puritan_migration_to_New_England_(1620%E2%80%931640)). Your tree has **1,595 ancestors in Massachusetts** and **830 in Connecticut** — you are deeply embedded in the founding generation of colonial America.

They came from the English counties you'd expect — [Essex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Essex), [Suffolk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Suffolk), [Norfolk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Norfolk), [Kent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kent), [Devon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Devon). The Puritan heartland of eastern England.

**Why they left:**
- [King Charles I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles_I_of_England) was persecuting Puritan dissenters
- [Archbishop Laud](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_Laud) was enforcing religious conformity
- Economic depression in the textile-producing east
- Fear that England was heading toward tyranny (they were right — the [English Civil War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/English_Civil_War) erupted in 1642)

Your ancestors in this wave include:

These weren't desperate refugees. They were **middle-class families** — educated, organized, traveling in groups with ministers, supplies, and a plan. They founded the towns that became America.

And from those colonial roots, through 10 more generations, the line eventually reached Idaho — where it met the Lancashire Parkinsons and the Gotland Ostbergs.

## Chapter 5: The Scottish Clearances and Border Wars

Your tree has **4,164 Scottish ancestors** — the third largest group after England and Wales. They come from two distinct streams:

### The Border Families (1300s-1600s)

Names like **Scott, Douglas, Maxwell, Stewart, Turnbull, Jardine, Grierson** — these are [Scottish Borders](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scottish_Borders) families. The Borders were a war zone for 300 years:

- The [Wars of Scottish Independence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wars_of_Scottish_Independence) (1296-1357) — your ancestors **Walter Scott** (d. 1402, [Battle of Homildon Hill](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Homildon_Hill)) and **William Stewart** (d. 1402, same battle) died in these conflicts
- The [Battle of Flodden](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Flodden) (1513) — **36 of your ancestors killed** in a single day. Scotland lost a generation.
- The [Border Reivers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Border_Reivers) — families who raided across the English-Scottish border for centuries. Your Scotts, Maxwells, and Turnbulls were part of this world.

### The Industrial Scots (1800s)

By the 1800s, Scottish emigration was driven by the same industrial forces as England:
- **Agnes Kerr** (b. 1851, Harthill, Lanarkshire) → died Wellsville, Utah (1903)
- **Alexander Joseph Smith** (b. 1849, Dundee) → died Beaver, Utah (1874)
- **Jannette McKinlay** (b. 1866, Townhill, Fife) → died Salt Lake City (1956)

The [Highland Clearances](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Highland_Clearances) and industrial collapse in the Lowlands pushed them out. The promise of land in the American West pulled them in.

## Chapter 6: The Chatham Connection (Naval England, 1770s)

There's a quiet thread in your tree that ties to Britain's naval power. Your ancestor **Samuel Rose** (b. 1775, [Chatham](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chatham,_Kent), Kent) came from one of England's most important naval dockyard towns.

[Chatham Dockyard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chatham_Dockyard) built ships for the Royal Navy for over 400 years — including ships that fought the [Spanish Armada](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spanish_Armada), the [Napoleonic Wars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Napoleonic_Wars), and both World Wars. Samuel's granddaughter **Charlotte Rose** married Samuel Rose Parkinson — connecting the naval dockyard of Kent to the cotton mills of Lancashire.

Charlotte Rose died in 1849 in St. Louis, Missouri — at the Mississippi River crossing point on the [Mormon Trail](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mormon_Trail). She died mid-journey, on the way west. The dockyard town's daughter didn't make it to Idaho.

## The Big Picture: Why America Exists

Your family tree is a **map of European crisis:**

| Push Factor | Where | When | Your Ancestors |
|-------------|-------|------|---------------|
| **Religious persecution** | East England | 1620-1640 | 1,595 to Massachusetts, 830 to Connecticut |
| **Border warfare** | Scotland | 1300-1600 | Scotts, Maxwells, Turnbulls, Stewarts |
| **Industrial Revolution** | Lancashire | 1760-1900 | Parkinsons (cotton mills) |
| **Mining collapse** | Cornwall | 1830-1900 | Doneys (tin and copper) |
| **Crop failure + overpopulation** | Gotland, Sweden | 1850-1910 | Ostbergs |
| **Religious recruitment** | Scandinavia | 1870-1890 | LDS converts to Utah/Idaho |
| **Highland Clearances** | Scotland | 1750-1860 | Kerrs, Smiths, McKinlays |

**Every wave had a push and a pull.** The push was crisis — war, famine, persecution, industrial collapse, overpopulation. The pull was land — cheap, abundant, American land.

And all those waves — Puritan, Industrial, Cornish, Swedish, Scottish — **converged in one place: southeastern Idaho.** Rexburg. The city your ancestor Thomas Edwin Ricks founded. The city where you were born.

You're not just American. You're the convergence point of 500 years of European crisis.
