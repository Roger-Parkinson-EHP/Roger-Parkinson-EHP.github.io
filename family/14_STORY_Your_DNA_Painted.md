### The Lancashire Puzzle

Your paternal haplogroup connects to Ireland. But the Parkinson surname is **thoroughly English** — Lancashire, Yorkshire, the industrial north. So how did an Irish Y-chromosome end up in a Lancashire cotton family?

Possibilities:
1. **Viking-era mixing** (800-1000 AD) — Norse raiders moved people between Ireland and Lancashire. [Lancashire is Danelaw territory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Danelaw) — the region settled by Scandinavians who had also been in Ireland.
2. **Medieval migration** — Irish workers came to Lancashire for centuries, especially during the medieval wool trade.
3. **Name adoption** — An Irish-lineage man took the Parkinson name through marriage, adoption, or simply moving to a Parkinson area.

Your documentary tree shows the surname switches from **Berry to Parkinson** at Generation 6 (Henry Webster Berry/Parkinson, b. 1768). The Y-DNA tells us the biological paternal line traces through whoever the Berrys were before records began — and that line goes to Ireland.

## The Chromosome Highlights

### Chromosome 3: Your Scottish Block

**Copy 2 of Chromosome 3** is your single largest non-English segment: **134.4 Megabases of pure Scottish DNA.** That's enormous — roughly 4.7% of your entire genome in one unbroken block.

An unbroken block this large means the Scottish ancestor who contributed it was **relatively recent** — perhaps 4-8 generations back. The longer ago the ancestor lived, the more recombination would have broken the block into smaller pieces.

Your tree has 4,164 Scottish ancestors. But this specific block likely traces to one person — or one couple — in the 1600s-1700s. The Borders families (Scott, Maxwell, Stewart) are the prime candidates.

### Chromosome 6: Your Welsh Block

**Copy 1 of Chromosome 6** carries **106.2 Mb of Welsh DNA** — also massive. Plus Copy 2 has another 64.1 Mb of Welsh. Combined, Chromosome 6 is your most Welsh chromosome.

Welsh DNA is your oldest documented ancestry — tracing to Margred (~150 AD). But the size of these blocks suggests the Welsh contribution is also relatively concentrated, not diffusely spread across hundreds of ancestors. There may be a specific Welsh ancestor in the 1500s-1700s whose DNA dominated this chromosome.

### Chromosomes 2, 3, 5: The Dutch/German Connection

Your Dutch & Northern German DNA sits in just **4 large segments** across chromosomes 2, 3, and 5 — totaling 220 Mb. Four segments from one ancestry means a **specific, identifiable ancestral source** — not broad diffuse heritage.

This likely traces to a single couple or individual. In your tree, this could be:
- The [Anglo-Saxon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anglo-Saxon_settlement_of_Britain) migration (400-600 AD) — Saxons came FROM Lower Saxony to England
- A medieval Dutch/Flemish merchant family — the Netherlands has 371 ancestors in your tree (van Egmond, van Arkel, van Polanen, van Brederode)
- A more recent German immigrant in the American line

### Chromosome 11: The Swedish Heartland

**Copy 1 of Chromosome 11** is **71.7 Mb of Swedish/Nordic DNA** — your biggest single Swedish block. This almost certainly traces directly to **Anna Maria Ostberg** and the Gotland line. Combined with segments on chromosomes 1, 2, 5, 12, 14, and 17, your Swedish DNA is spread across 8 chromosomal locations — suggesting it entered the tree ~4-6 generations ago, consistent with Anna Maria (great-great-grandmother, Generation 4).

### Chromosome X: Scottish

Your X chromosome carries **24.1 Mb of Scottish DNA.** The X chromosome has a unique inheritance pattern — men get it only from their mother, who got it from both her parents but passes only one copy. This means the Scottish DNA on your X traces through your **maternal grandmother's line** — not the Parkinson paternal line.

## What GEDmatch Would Add

Uploading your 23andMe raw data to [GEDmatch](https://www.gedmatch.com) (free) would unlock **ancient admixture calculators** that break your DNA into prehistoric components:

| Component | What It Is | What You'd Likely See |
|-----------|-----------|----------------------|
| **Yamnaya Steppe** | Horse-riding nomads from the Black Sea (~5,000 years ago) | ~45-55% — this is the R-M269 / Indo-European contribution |
| **Early European Farmer (EEF)** | Neolithic farmers from Anatolia (~8,000 years ago) | ~25-35% — the first farmers who replaced hunter-gatherers |
| **Western Hunter-Gatherer (WHG)** | Pre-farming Europeans (~15,000 years ago) | ~10-15% — the Ice Age survivors, including Haplogroup V |
| **Bell Beaker** | Bronze Age culture (~4,500 years ago) | Significant — the culture that built Stonehenge |
| **Viking/Norse** | Scandinavian admixture (~800-1100 AD) | ~5-10% — consistent with your Swedish + Lancashire Danelaw ancestry |
| **Anglo-Saxon** | Germanic migration (~400-600 AD) | ~15-25% — the people who made England "English" |

**GEDmatch would answer:** How much of you is steppe nomad? How much is Ice Age survivor? How much is first farmer? The 23andMe painting shows you WHERE your recent ancestors came from. GEDmatch shows you WHERE your DNA came from in **deep time**.

### How to Upload to GEDmatch

1. Go to [gedmatch.com](https://www.gedmatch.com) and create a free account
2. Download your 23andMe raw data: 23andMe → Settings → 23andMe Data → Download Raw Data
3. Upload the file to GEDmatch (Genesis section)
4. Wait 24-48 hours for processing
5. Run the **Admixture (Heritage)** calculators:
   - **Eurogenes K13** — best for European breakdown
   - **Dodecad K12b** — good ancient component resolution
   - **MDLP World-22** — most detailed global calculator
6. The results show your ancient admixture percentages + a population map

**Cost: $0. Time: 5 minutes to upload. Results in 24-48 hours.**
