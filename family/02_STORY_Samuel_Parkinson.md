## The Story

Your Parkinson line stretches back nine generations to the mist-shrouded hills of Lancashire, England — and the story it tells is one of the most dramatic migration narratives in your family tree.

### The Pendle Roots (1675-1831)

Your earliest documented Parkinson ancestor was born around **1675 in Lancashire, England.** His descendants lived for generations in and around **[Newchurch in Pendle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newchurch-in-Pendle)** — a name that should send a chill down your spine.

Newchurch in Pendle sits in the shadow of [Pendle Hill](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pendle_Hill), a place forever marked by the **[Pendle Witch Trials of 1612](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pendle_witches)** — one of the most famous witch trials in English history. Twelve people from the Pendle area were accused of murder by witchcraft. Ten were hanged at [Lancaster Castle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lancaster_Castle). Your ancestor **James Parkinson** (b. 1733) was born at "Foothouse Gate, Newchurch in Pendle" — just 121 years after the trials, in a community that still bore the weight of that dark chapter.

By the time James was raising his family, Pendle was transforming. The [Industrial Revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Industrial_Revolution) was arriving in Lancashire. [Cotton mills](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lancashire_cotton_industry) were rising along the rivers. Your ancestor **Henry Webster Parkinson** (b. 1768) grew up as [Barrowford](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barrowford) — the village just down the road — was becoming a cotton mill town. The Samuel Holden cotton mill would eventually dominate the village, and the seven locks on the [Leeds and Liverpool Canal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leeds_and_Liverpool_Canal) nearby moved goods between the industrial heartlands.

Henry's son **William Parkinson** (b. 1800, Briercliffe) died young — just 31 years old in 1831, the same year his son was born. That son would change everything.

### Samuel Rose Parkinson: The Immigrant (1831-1919)

**Samuel Rose Parkinson** was born on April 12, 1831, in Barrowford, Lancashire. His father William died when Samuel was an infant. His mother Charlotte Rose had been born in Chatham, Kent — a naval dockyard town on the other side of England.

Samuel grew up in a Lancashire that was both booming and brutal. The cotton mills ran day and night. Child labor was common. Cholera swept through industrial towns. But sometime in the mid-1800s, Samuel made a decision that would redirect your family's entire future: **he left England for America.**

He married Charlotte Elizabeth Smart (b. 1849, St. Louis, Missouri) — a woman born in the heart of the American frontier. And together, they settled in **[Franklin, Idaho](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Franklin,_Idaho)** — one of the earliest settlements in Idaho Territory, founded in 1860 by Mormon pioneers just a mile north of the Utah border.

Samuel lived to be 88 years old, dying on May 23, 1919, in **Preston, Franklin County, Idaho.** He'd traveled from a cotton mill village in the shadow of Pendle Hill to the wide-open valleys of southeastern Idaho. From a place haunted by witch trials to a place that didn't even exist as a state until 1890.

### The Idaho Generations (1875-present)

Samuel's son **Frederick Smart Parkinson** (b. 1875, Franklin, Idaho) was born into the new world his father had chosen. Frederick married Bessie Ann Doney — whose family came from **[Cornwall](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cornwall), England** — and settled in [Rexburg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rexburg,_Idaho), Madison County, Idaho.

Their son **Keith Doney Parkinson** (b. 1912, Rexburg) lived his entire life in eastern Idaho, dying in Rexburg in 1998 at age 85. His son **Keith Roberts "Bob" Parkinson** (b. 1937, Rexburg) died younger — just 59, in 1996 in St. Anthony.

And then: **Roger Parkinson**, born March 14, 1975, in Rexburg, Madison, Idaho.

## Historical Context

### Barrowford, Lancashire
- **Coordinates:** 53.851°N, 2.221°W
- **Population:** ~6,000
- Village in the **Pendle district** of Lancashire, near the Forest of Bowland
- On the **Marsden-Gisburn-Long Preston turnpike** — a coaching road
- Near the **Leeds and Liverpool Canal** with its famous seven-lock staircase
- Home to cotton mills including the **Samuel Holden cotton mill**
- The packhorse bridge near Higherford Mill dates to the late 1500s

### The Pendle Witch Trials (1612)
In 1612, twelve people from the Pendle area of Lancashire were accused of the murder of ten people by the use of witchcraft. Ten of the accused were tried at Lancaster Assizes and hanged. The trials are among the most famous and best-recorded witch trials in English history. Newchurch in Pendle — where your Parkinson ancestors lived — was at the geographic center of these events.

### Lancashire Cotton Industry (1760-1860)
Lancashire became the center of the world's cotton industry during the Industrial Revolution. Mills powered by water and then steam transformed rural villages into industrial towns. Workers — including children — labored 12-16 hour days. Living conditions were often desperate.
